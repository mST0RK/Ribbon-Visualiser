It may have been sometime in mid-January of this year when I first raised my eyes and noticed the spot upon the wall. To attach a date to anything one has to summon back the things visible around it. So I recall the fire now; the quiet wash of yellow light spread over the page I was reading; the three chrysanthemums standing in the round glass vase on the mantel.

Yes, it must have been winter, and tea must just have ended, because I remember holding a cigarette when I glanced up and saw the spot on the wall for the first time. I looked through the smoke from the cigarette, and my gaze rested for an instant on the glowing coal, and that familiar old invention of the red banner stirring from a castle turret came back to me, and I saw again the procession of scarlet knights climbing the black cliff.

The sight of the mark, rather thankfully, broke off the scene, since that imagining is an old one, one of those automatic inventions perhaps formed in childhood. The spot was little and round, dark against the white of the wall, perhaps six or seven inches above the mantelpiece.

How quickly the mind gathers round a new object, raising it for a little while, just as ants hurry under a straw, then desert it.... If the spot had been made by a nail, it cannot have held a picture; it would have been for a miniature—the likeness of a lady with powdered white curls, cheeks pale with powder, and lips red as carnations. A sham, naturally, for the people who lived here before us would have selected pictures like that—an ancient picture for an ancient room.

That was the kind of people they seemed to be—oddly interesting people, and I think about them often, and in strange places, because one will never meet them again, never learn what followed.

They wished to leave this house, so he explained, because they meant to alter their furniture; and he was just developing his view that art ought to have ideas behind it when we were torn apart, as one is torn from the old woman about to pour tea and the young man lifting his racquet to strike a tennis ball in the back garden of some suburban villa when the train rushes by.

But about the mark itself, I am uncertain; I do not think a nail caused it after all; it is too large and too round for that. I could get up, but if I got up and examined it, ten chances to one I still should not be certain, because once a thing has happened, no one ever really knows how it happened.

Oh dear, the mystery of existence; the imprecision of thought! The ignorance of human beings! To show how little mastery we have over what belongs to us—how accidental, after all our civilization, this business of living remains—let me count a few of the things lost in one lifetime, starting with what is always most mysterious among losses—what cat would chew, what rat would gnaw—three pale blue tins of bookbinding instruments? Then the bird-cages, the iron hoops, the steel skates, the Queen Anne coal-scuttle, the bagatelle board, the barrel organ—all vanished, and jewels too.

Opals and emeralds, for all we know, are lying round the roots of turnips. What a scraped, pared matter it is! It is astonishing that I have clothes on my body, that at this very moment I sit among furniture that appears solid.

If one wants a comparison for life, one must compare it to being driven through the Tube at fifty miles an hour and flung out at the other end without a single hairpin left in one's hair. Ejected at the feet of God stark naked! Rolled head over heels among the asphodel meadows like brown-paper parcels shot down a chute in a post office! One's hair streamed backward like the tail of a racehorse.

Yes, that seems to catch the speed of living, the endless waste and repair; everything casual, everything thrown together....

But after life? The slow bending down of thick green stems until the cup of the flower turns and drenches one in purple and crimson light. Why, after all, should one not be born there as one is born here—helpless, speechless, unable to focus the eyes, feeling blindly among grass roots, among the toes of Giants? As for deciding which are trees and which are men and women, or whether such things exist at all, one would not be fit to do that for fifty years or so.

There would be only spaces of brightness and darkness, crossed by thick stalks, and perhaps higher up, flower-like smudges of vague colour—faint pinks and blues—which, with time, would become firmer, become—I do not know what....

And yet the mark on the wall is not a hole at all. It may even have been made by some small dark round thing, perhaps a little rose leaf left from summer; and I, being no especially watchful housekeeper—look, for instance, at the dust on the mantelpiece, dust that, people say, buried Troy three times over, leaving only shards of pots obstinately refusing to be annihilated, as one can well believe.

The tree beyond the window taps very softly on the glass.... I want to think quietly, calmly, largely, never to be interrupted, never to rise from my chair, to glide from one thing to another without any feeling of resistance or hostility. I want to sink lower and lower, away from the surface with its hard separate facts. To steady myself, let me seize the first thought that passes.... Shakespeare.... Well, he will serve as well as any.

A man settling himself firmly in an armchair, staring into the fire, like this—A shower of ideas perpetually descended from some very high heaven through his mind. He leaned his forehead on his hand, while people, looking in through the open door—for the scene is imagined on a summer evening—But how tedious this is, this historical invention! It does not interest me at all.

I wish I could strike a pleasant course of thought, a course that indirectly reflects credit on myself, since those are the most agreeable thoughts, and very common even in modest, mouse-coloured people who sincerely believe they do not like to hear themselves praised. They are not thoughts that praise oneself directly; that is their charm; they are thoughts rather like this:

"Then I entered the room. They were talking about botany. I said that I had seen a flower growing on a rubbish heap where an old house had stood in Kingsway. The seed, I said, must have been dropped there in the reign of Charles the First. What flowers grew in the reign of Charles the First?" I asked—though I cannot remember the answer. Tall flowers perhaps, with purple tassels. And so it continues.

All the while I am secretly and fondly dressing up the figure of myself inside my own mind, though not openly worshipping it, because if I did that I should detect myself at once and reach for a book in self-defence. It is curious, indeed, how instinctively one shields the image of oneself from idolatry, or from any handling that might make it absurd, or too far unlike the original to be believed in any longer. Or perhaps it is not curious at all. It matters greatly.

Suppose the mirror breaks, the image vanishes, and the romantic figure surrounded by the green depths of forest is gone, leaving only that shell of a person seen by other people—what a shallow, airless, bare, glaring world it becomes! A world impossible to inhabit, because it has no secret interior left in it, no soft darkness where the self may continue its private ceremonies. When we face one another in omnibuses and underground trains we are looking into the mirror; that explains the vagueness, the glassy brightness, in our eyes.

And future novelists will understand more and more how important these reflections are, because of course there is not one reflection but an almost infinite number. Those are the depths they will explore, those the phantoms they will follow, leaving the description of reality increasingly outside their stories, assuming knowledge of it as the Greeks did and perhaps Shakespeare—but these generalizations are entirely valueless. The military note of the word is enough.

It calls back leading articles, cabinet ministers—a whole class of things, indeed, which as a child one took for the thing itself, the standard thing, the actual thing, from which departure was possible only at the risk of some nameless damnation. Generalizations somehow bring back Sunday in London, Sunday afternoon walks, Sunday luncheons, and also the proper ways of speaking about the dead, clothes, habits—the habit, for instance, of sitting together in one room until a certain hour, though nobody cared for it.

There used to be a rule for everything. The rule for tablecloths at that period was that they had to be made of tapestry, with little yellow compartments marked on them, such as one may see in photographs of carpets in the passages of royal palaces. Other sorts of tablecloths were not real tablecloths; they were makeshifts, mistakes, things somehow not admitted into the solemn company of established objects.

How startling, and yet how marvellous, it was to discover that these real things—Sunday luncheons, Sunday walks, country houses, tablecloths—were not wholly real, were in fact half phantoms, and that the damnation awaiting those who disbelieved in them was no more than a sense of unlawful freedom.

What takes the place of such things now, I wonder, those standard real things? Men perhaps, if one is a woman; the masculine point of view that governs our lives, sets the measure, establishes Whitaker's Table of Precedency, and has, I suppose, since the war, become half a phantom for many men and women, and may soon, one hopes, be laughed into the dustbin where phantoms go—the mahogany sideboards, the Landseer prints, Gods and Devils, Hell and so on—leaving us all with an intoxicating sense of unlawful freedom, if freedom exists....

In certain lights the mark on the wall seems actually to stand out from the wall. Nor is it perfectly round. I cannot be certain, but it appears to throw a small shadow, suggesting that if I ran my finger down that strip of wall it would at one point rise and fall over a little mound, a smooth mound like those barrows on the South Downs which are said to be either graves or camps.

Of the two, I should prefer graves, wanting melancholy like most English people, and finding it natural at the end of a walk to think of bones lying stretched under the grass.... There must be some book on the subject. Some antiquary must have dug up those bones and assigned them a name....

What sort of person is an antiquary, I wonder? Retired Colonels, mostly, I dare say, leading groups of elderly labourers up to the summit, inspecting clods of earth and stones, and entering into correspondence with neighbouring clergymen; letters which, when opened at breakfast, give them a sense of importance, while comparison of arrowheads requires journeys across country to county towns, an agreeable necessity both to them and to their elderly wives, who want to make plum jam or clear out the study and have every reason to keep the large question of camp or tomb indefinitely suspended, while the Colonel himself feels pleasantly philosophical as he gathers evidence on both sides.

It is true that at last he inclines to the belief that it was a camp; and, when contradicted, composes a pamphlet which he is about to read at the quarterly meeting of the local society when a stroke lays him low, and his last conscious thoughts are not of wife or child but of the camp and that arrowhead over there, now displayed in the local museum beside the foot of a Chinese murderess, a handful of Elizabethan nails, many Tudor clay pipes, a fragment of Roman pottery, and the wineglass Nelson drank from—proving, I really cannot say what.

No, no, nothing is proved, nothing is known.

And if I were to rise this instant and discover that the mark on the wall is really—what shall we call it?—the head of some enormous old nail, driven there two hundred years ago and now, after the patient rubbing of generations of housemaids, uncovered above the coat of paint and taking its first look at modern life in a white-walled room lit by firelight, what would I gain? Knowledge? Material for further speculation? I can think as well sitting still as standing up.

And what is knowledge? What are learned men except the descendants of witches and hermits who crouched in caves and woods, boiling herbs, questioning shrew-mice, and writing down the language of the stars? And the less we honour them as our superstitions diminish and our respect for beauty and sanity increases.... Yes, one could imagine a very pleasant world. A quiet, ample world, with flowers so red and blue in the open fields.

A world without professors or specialists or housekeepers with policemen's profiles; a world through which thought could cut as a fish cuts water with its fin, brushing the stems of water-lilies, hanging motionless above nests of white sea-eggs....

How peaceful it is down here, rooted at the centre of the world and looking upward through grey waters, with their sudden flashes of light and their reflections—if only there were no Whitaker's Almanack—if only there were no Table of Precedency!

I must leap up and find out for myself what that mark on the wall actually is—a nail, a rose leaf, a crack in the wood?

Here nature is again at her old trick of self-preservation. This train of thought, she sees, is threatening simple waste of energy, perhaps even some collision with reality; for who will ever raise a finger against Whitaker's Table of Precedency? The Archbishop of Canterbury comes after one name and before another; the Lord High Chancellor follows; the Archbishop of York follows after him. Everyone follows someone; such is Whitaker's philosophy, and the important thing is knowing who follows whom.

Whitaker knows, and let that, Nature advises, console you instead of enraging you; and if you cannot be consoled, if you insist on breaking this hour of quiet, then think about the mark on the wall.

I understand Nature's device—her push toward action as a means of cutting short any thought that threatens to disturb or hurt. From this, I suppose, comes our slight contempt for men of action—men whom we presume do not think. Still, there is no harm in ending disagreeable thoughts by looking at a mark on the wall.

Indeed, now that I have fixed my eyes on it, I feel as though I had caught hold of a plank in the sea; I feel the satisfying pressure of reality, which at once turns the two Archbishops and the Lord High Chancellor into shadows of shadows. Here is something certain, something actual, something that refuses to arrange itself into rank or argument, and merely holds the eye.

So, waking from a dreadful dream at midnight, one quickly turns on the light and lies still, adoring the chest of drawers, adoring solidity, adoring reality, adoring the impersonal world that proves some existence beyond our own. That is what one wants to be sure of.... Wood is a pleasant thing to think about. It comes from a tree; and trees grow, and we do not know how they grow, although all our explanations flutter round them like labels tied to branches.

For years upon years they grow without attending to us, in meadows, in forests, and beside rivers—all things one likes to think of. Cows flick their tails beneath them on hot afternoons; they colour the rivers so green that when a moorhen dives one expects its feathers to come up green too. I like to think of fish poised against the current like flags blown straight out, and of water beetles slowly lifting little domes of mud from the riverbed.

I like to think of the tree itself: first the close dry sensation of being wood; then the grinding force of storm; then the slow, delicious rising of sap. I like to think of it also on winter nights, standing in an empty field with every leaf folded tight, nothing tender exposed to the iron bullets of the moon, a bare mast on an earth that goes tumbling, tumbling, through the whole night.

The birds' song must sound very loud and strange in June; and how cold the feet of insects must feel as they make their laborious way up the bark's creases, or sun themselves beneath the thin green awning of leaves, staring ahead with diamond-cut red eyes.... One by one the fibres snap under the immense cold pressure of the earth; then the last storm comes, and falling, the highest branches drive deep into the ground once more.

Even then, life has not finished with it; a tree still has a million patient, watchful lives throughout the world—in bedrooms, in ships, on pavements, lining rooms where men and women sit after tea and smoke cigarettes. This tree is full of peaceful thoughts, happy thoughts. I should like to take each separately—but something is obstructing me.... Where was I? What has all this been about? A tree? A river? The Downs? Whitaker's Almanack? The asphodel fields? I cannot remember anything.

Everything is moving, slipping, dropping, disappearing.... There is an immense upheaval of matter. Someone is standing over me and saying—

"I'm going out to buy a newspaper."

"Yes?"

"Though there's no use buying newspapers.... Nothing ever happens. Curse this war; God damn this war!... Still, I don't see why we should have a snail on our wall."

Ah, the mark on the wall! It was a snail.
